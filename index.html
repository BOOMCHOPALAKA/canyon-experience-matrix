Canyon Experience Matrix

Every technical canyon in Washington, in one sortable table. It pulls from RopeWiki and scores each one so you can size up a day at a glance.

RopeWiki describes the approach and exit for every canyon, but it's prose. You read it one canyon at a time, and there's no number to compare. The two things you can sort by don't fill the gap either: the ACA rating covers the canyoneering itself, and the star rating is anyone's guess. One person's 5 might be weighing an easy approach as much as the canyoneering itself, while the next person's is purely about the canyon, and you can't tell which from a number. So this reads the approach and exit beta and scores it the same way for every canyon: a number on each, rolled into one Access score. The ACA rating gets broken into its three real parts (technical, water, and time) instead of just the leading digit. And there's a seasonal wasp score: how much nesting ground you cross on the way in and out, plus any nests people have actually reported.

Click any column header to sort. Hover the ⓘ on a column to see exactly how it's figured. Click a canyon name for its full RopeWiki beta.

All canyon data sourced from RopeWiki, the community canyoneering wiki. Data as of Jul 24, 2026

What's scored

Access: approach + exit effort (ours, 1-5)
ACA: technical · water · time (theirs, official)
Wasp: nesting terrain, hover for why (seasonal)
Adventure = ACA technical + Access

Access scale · 1 → 5

1 Walking trail to the water, under a mile
2 Hiking still a trail, but longer or steeper
3 Off-trail the trail runs out partway
4 Bushwhacking sustained, a bad section
5 Suffering long bushwhack, in the thick of it
